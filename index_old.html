I'm currently a fourth year Ph.D. student in the ARC group, at the Computer Science department in Johns Hopkins University, advised by Abhishek Jain. I'm also affiliated with the Theory group.

I spent the summer of 2018 working with Krzysztof Pietrzak at IST Austria, and the summer of 2019 working with Nir Bitansky at Tel Aviv University.

Prior to starting my Ph.D., I completed my masters degree in Computer Science at Indian Statistical Institute Kolkata, working with Subhamoy Maitra on some aspects of symmetric cryptography. At the time, I was hosted by Serge Vaudenay during the summer of 2015, where I worked with Divesh Aggarwal. My undergraduate degree was in Computer Science and Engineering from National Institute of Technology Karnataka, Surathkal.

My primary research interests lie in the various aspects of Cryptography, and some other aspects of theoretical computer science.

In my relatively short time doing research, I've been extremely fortunate to have worked with an incredible set of people on various projects.

  • A New Approach to Round-Optimal Secure Multiparty Computation [eprint][show summary]
    Prabhanjan Ananth, Arka Rai Choudhuri, Abhishek Jain
    CRYPTO 2017

    In this work, we look at the number of communication rounds required for Muliparty computation (MPC) where only a minority of participants may be honest. Specifically, we look to establish the tightness of known bounds in the domain: [Garg-Mukherjee-Pandey-Polychroniadou'16] established a lower bound showing no three round protocol can exist, and an upper bound by constructing a five round protocol under the strong assumption of the existence of indistinguishability obfuscation (iO); the iO assumption is unnatural given that it is not known to be required for MPC protocols. As a first step, we show that one can in fact construct five round protocols based only the mild assumption that Decisional Diffie-Hellman (DDH) is hard. Further, we show that the lower bound is tight by constructing a four round protocol under the (significantly stronger) assumption that DDH remains hard even when an adversary trying to break it is allowed to run in time sub-exponential in the security parameter.

    Our techniques in the construction of these protocols depart significantly from prior works in this domain. We hark back to the age old technique in [Goldreich-Micali-Wigderson'87] of each party proving honest behavior for every message it sends, but with a necessary twist. Without any additional set-up, zero-knowledge proofs require 4 rounds of interaction. Thereby, in order to squeeze in our protocol into 4 (or even 5) rounds, we construct a 3 round protocol satisfying a seemingly week privacy notion that we call robustness: privacy is maintained Cumulative proof


  • Significantly Improved Multi-bit Differentials for Reduced Round Salsa and ChaCha [eprint][show summary]
    Arka Rai Choudhuri, Subhamoy Maitra
    FSE 2017 / ToSC 2016

    • In this work, we study how changes in a single bit of the starting cipher state of Salsa and ChaCha propagates biases in other bits of the cipher state. While these biases dissipate in just a few rounds of these ciphers, we show that by carefully choosing a combination of bits from the state, one is able to see strong biases that had previously been missed. These biases are then exploited to show weaknesses in the reduced round version of Salsa and ChaCha, improving the best known key recovery attacks on 8 rounds of Salsa and 7 round of ChaCha, and for the first time providing practical attacks on 6 rounds of Salsa and 5 rounds of ChaCha. Our observations can be viewed through the lens of differential-linear cryptanalysis and we present it as such in the paper.
